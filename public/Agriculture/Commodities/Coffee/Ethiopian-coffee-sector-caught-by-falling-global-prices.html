Eastern Africa , Coffee

Ethiopian coffee sector caught by falling global prices

22 July 2013

Ethiopia is Africa’s largest coffee producer and the fifth largest producer in the world, producing mainly arabica coffee. Production takes place primarily on smallholder farms (95%), under traditional production and farm management systems. These range from wild and semi-wild collection to managed smallholder farming operations. Production, according to a recent report by the USDA, is “characterized by limited use of fertilizers and pesticides with manual cultivation and drying processes”. Production per hectare is very low, with no specialised government institution providing extension support for coffee production.

While traders claim that Ethiopian coffee is organically produced, it is not officially certified as such by internationally recognised bodies. Nevertheless, Ethiopian coffee is “in high demand in the international markets, and it is specifically valued for its special aroma and distinct flavor”.

USDA notes that Ethiopia is one of the largest consumers of coffee in Africa, with nearly half of its production being consumed nationally. Prices of coffee are usually higher on the local market, with the government banning sales of export-grade coffee on the local market.  

According to USDA, nearly all coffee is sold on the government-established Ethiopia Commodity Exchange (ECX), either directly through coffee producers’ cooperatives or intermediaries. The ECX is “a public market facilitating institution”, and was set up mainly “to eliminate the huge number of middlemen involved in coffee distribution and to enable coffee farmers to benefit from prevailing market prices”. However, USDA has noted that “coffee sold through ECX is considered as commodity coffee and will not get the possible premiums of being organic coffee.” Ethiopia mainly exports “green beans with only a very small amount of roasted beans”.

The USDA report notes that the coffee sector generates around 25% of Ethiopia’s export earnings: trade in coffee is regulated with the aim of maximising foreign exchange. All coffee-sector-related enterprises require special licences to operate, and a government regulation introduced in May 2011 limits the amount of stock an exporter can store without having a shipment contract.

While the increase in production for the current marketing year (MY) 2012/13 was marginal, USDA observes that large volumes of coffee have been exported “as a result of huge stocks carried over from last year’s production”. The carry-over of stocks followed a government directive in November 2011 “requiring coffee traders to ship coffee in bulk containers rather than using traditional 60-kg jute bags”. This disruption to exports unfortunately meant that the elevated export volumes, after the withdrawal of the directive, “failed to generate a proportionate increase in foreign exchange earnings due to a substantial decline in international coffee prices”.

In June 2013, Agrimoney.com reported that the International Coffee Organization (ICO) had “cautioned that the weak market had cut prices below the cost of production”, with arabica coffee prices nearly halving in the past 2 years. The ICO maintained that changes in the structure of the global coffee market offered little prospect for a recovery in the arabica price premium, with patterns of consumption in emerging markets driving a stronger increase in demand for robusta coffee.  This has seen robusta prices fall less severely in the past 2 years (the average monthly price in May 2013 was 16.3% below the May 2011 peak level) than arabica prices (the average monthly price in May 2013 was 50% below the April 2011 peak level).

Editorial comment

There appears to be a sharp contrast between the experience of support to coffee production in the East African Community (EAC) (see companion Agritrade article ‘Good performance in the EAC’s coffee sector, despite depressed global prices’, forthcoming 2013) and the experience in Ethiopia. Notable differences relate to the government extension service support, replanting programmes and quality-based product differentiation. There are also differences in the role of government in regulating the trade.

The EAC coffee sector has been largely liberalised. Producers have a long history of organisation into cooperatives, gaining economies of scale. The production of coffee is fairly modernised, utilising adequate levels of fertilisers and agro-chemicals procured competitively through cooperatives. Processing is also liberalised, with farmers choosing where to mill their coffee. This has improved the efficiency of the sector, lowering costs and raising profitability. Currently in Kenya efforts are in progress to promote coffee consumption locally, with high-quality roasted coffee beans being vacuum packed for sale on the local Kenyan market. This experience of processing, packaging and marketing could potentially assist the Kenyan coffee sector in breaking into export markets for value-added coffee products.

Given the extent to which the USDA analysis suggests that Ethiopia was caught out by declines in coffee prices in 2012/13, there may well be lessons to be learnt from the Kenyan experience, which would enable Ethiopian coffee producers more consistently to obtain higher value in certain specific market components (e.g. organic markets, or the Japanese market, where there is a preference for unwashed coffee, which is held to provide a “better and richer taste”).
